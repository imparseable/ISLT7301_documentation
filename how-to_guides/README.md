# How-to guides
How-to guides are directions that guide a reader through a problem. They should be written from the user's perspective (meaning human), meaning there will be lots of "you" statements; they are not anthropomorphizations of a computer's actions.

## What a how-to guide is
How-to guides are *procedural*, meaning they are about solutions that involve steps that could overlap, or not be order-dependent. You have experienced this in troubleshooting, as the order in which files can be checked is linear, one at a time, and likely doesn't matter for the outcome; any and all of the troubleshooting may be necessary, so the how-to guide is inclusive of real-world, additional possibilities for applying guidance to a problem.

### The focus of the how-to guide
How-to guides are about tasks, work, or problems; all have practical goals. Maintain your focus on that goal. Explanations should be linked, as they are not part of the guidance.

## What a how-to guide address and include
- Naming
    - The title needs to be what the how-to guide illustrates.
      - "How to troubleshoot *mod_rewrite not enabled* in Omeka"
      - "How to fix WordPress pages not completely loading in HTML in-browser"
- Real-world complexity
  - the guide should be adaptable to different use-cases and scenarios
- Practical usability
  - How-tos are not end-to-end complete tutorials, they start somewhere reasonable (such as where something does not work and what the symptoms are), and ends at a meaningful place
- Sets of instructions
  - These are xecutable instructions, such as outlining steps (or actions) to take that the reader must align to their work. Instructions and actions require flow to be considered, such as what the user needs to think about and when, and at what steps.
- Descriptions of logical sequences
  - Sequential, meaning ordered in time, actions to take. These include commands as well as where the reader needs to engage in thinking the problem through; the guide addresses *how the user needs to think* AND *what the user needs to do**

## Language
Use a clear statement to describe what the how-to guide is going to show the reader how to solve. Use language such as "if you want x, do y." "If you are using *software a* version *a.1*, check that software *b* is version *b.1 or greater*."

Think of the how-to guide as a recipe. How would you bake a cake? We're assuming some basic competence and the ability to follow directions.
